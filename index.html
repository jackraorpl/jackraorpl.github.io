Research Statement

My research agenda is focused on a central challenge in modern empirical economics: ensuring the credibility of economic inference in complex, high-dimensional settings. As our models and data grow in complexity, how do we guarantee our conclusions are robust, credible, and economically meaningful?

I pursue this question from two complementary angles. From an "outside-in" perspective, I apply cutting-edge causal machine learning to test the structural predictions of economic theory, such as the (non-linear) liquidity spiral mechanisms responsible for (linear) market trough boundaries. Concurrently, from an "inside-out," theory-first perspective, I develop and apply novel computational methods (like Deep BSDE and Dynamic Programming) to solve high-dimensional structural models (e.g., Bayesian HJB, HANK) that were previously intractable, connecting a model's architecture to its economic plausibility.

This dual approach positions my work at the intersection of computational economics, empirical asset pricing, and machine learning, with the goal of building more rigorous and reliable tools for economic analysis.

Working Papers

Market Troughs as Bifurcation Events: A Liquidity Spiral Framework and Empirical Validation

Peilin (Jack) Rao, Randall Rojas

Extended Version of NeurIPS 2025 CauScien Workshop paper.

Why do linear models perform well at predicting V-shaped market troughs (ROC AUC = 0.9435), but the underlying theory (liquidity spirals) is inherently non-linear? We show that this is a false dichotomy and demonstrate that the canonical Brunnermeier & Pedersen (2009) model implies both a partially linear bifurcation boundary and non-linear structural interactions (spirals) that drive the market state. We provide empirical evidence for each structure: a linear SVM (the correct tool for the boundary) outperforms non-linear classifiers, while a non-linear DML-APE (the correct tool for the spirals) succeeds in identifying the key drivers of market fragility, whereas a restrictive linear causal model produces spurious results and consistently misidentifies the direction of critical risk drivers. Our research provides strong validation of the Brunnermeier (2009) mechanism by demonstrating that its linear and non-linear elements are both observable in the data.

The GNN as a Low-Pass Filter: A Spectral Perspective on Achieving Stability in Neural PDE Solvers

Peilin (Jack) Rao

Proceedings of the NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on New Perspectives in Graph Machine Learning (NPGML), San Diego, USA.

This work provides a theoretical contribution to the use of deep learning for solving high-dimensional optimal control problems (HJB equations). I formally prove that the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) operator functions as a spectral low-pass filter. This provides a novel explanation for its stability and introduces the concept of "architectural bias as implicit Lipschitz regularization," connecting a neural network's structure to the economic plausibility of the learned solution.

Research in Progress

A Bayesian HJB Framework for Robust Monetary Policy under Structural Uncertainty

Peilin (Jack) Rao

Deep structural uncertainty, exemplified by the unstable Phillips Curve, undermines single-model monetary policy. Existing paradigms are either brittle to misspecification (Certainty-Equivalence) or overly pessimistic and static (Worst-Case Robustness). This research proposes a Bayesian Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) framework that optimizes policy over a belief-weighted 'cloud' of models, which formalizes the dual control problem to balance stabilization with active learning. I solve this historically intractable problem using the Deep Backward Stochastic Differential Equation (Deep BSDE) method, overcoming the curse of dimensionality. The result is a tractable, adaptive policy demonstrably more robust to structural breaks than existing static approaches.

Future Research Interests

Advancing Causal Machine Learning in Finance

Building on my existing work, I plan to develop novel estimators for causal inference tailored to the unique challenges of financial data, such as non-stationarity, extreme events, and high feature correlation. Key questions include estimating heterogeneous causal effects across economic regimes and conducting valid inference on data with structural breaks.

Solving High-Dimensional Structural Models

My foundational work on GNNs and implicit regularization (NeurIPS 2025) and my proposal on Bayesian HJB models (using Deep BSDE) form the basis for my core agenda: solving the next generation of large-scale structural models. I am focused on applying deep learning and dynamic programming methods to solve previously intractable problems in computational economics, particularly Heterogeneous Agent New Keynesian (HANK) models and Mean-Field Games (MFGs).
