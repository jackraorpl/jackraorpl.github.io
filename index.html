Research Statement

My research agenda is focused on a central challenge in modern empirical economics: ensuring the credibility of economic inference in complex, high-dimensional settings. As our models and data grow in complexity, how do we guarantee our conclusions are robust, credible, and economically meaningful?

I pursue this question from two complementary angles. From an "outside-in" perspective, I apply cutting-edge, data-first causal inference methods to real-world financial data, demonstrating how crucial methodological choices are for valid economic conclusions. Concurrently, from an "inside-out," theory-first perspective, I develop the theoretical foundations for the next generation of structural models, proving how a model's architecture can provide the implicit regularization needed for stable and plausible solutions.

This dual approach positions my work at the intersection of empirical asset pricing, structural modeling, and machine learning, with the goal of building more rigorous and reliable tools for economic analysis.

Publications & Preprints

Predicting Market Troughs: A Machine Learning Approach with Causal Interpretation

Peilin (Jack) Rao, Randall Rojas

Submitted to The Review of Financial Studies. Submitted to the NeurIPS 2025 CauScien Workshop.

This paper develops a framework for "nowcasting" rare market troughs using high-performance classifiers and interprets the model's predictions through a causal lens. We employ Double/Debiased Machine Learning (DML) to estimate causal parameters in a high-dimensional setting and conduct formal sensitivity analysis for unobserved confounding, connecting reduced-form findings to structural theories like Intermediary Asset Pricing and Liquidity Spirals.

The GNN as a Low-Pass Filter: A Spectral Perspective on Achieving Stability in Neural PDE Solvers

Peilin (Jack) Rao

Working Paper. Submitted to the NeurIPS 2025 New Perspectives in Advancing Graph Machine Learning Workshop.

This work provides a theoretical contribution to the use of deep learning for solving high-dimensional optimal control problems (HJB equations). I formally prove that the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) operator functions as a spectral low-pass filter. This provides a novel explanation for its stability and introduces the concept of "architectural bias as implicit Lipschitz regularization," connecting a neural network's structure to the economic plausibility of the learned solution.

Future Research Interests

Advancing Causal Machine Learning in Finance

Building on my existing work, I plan to develop novel estimators for causal inference tailored to the unique challenges of financial data, such as non-stationarity, extreme events, and high feature correlation. Key questions include estimating heterogeneous causal effects across economic regimes and conducting valid inference on data with structural breaks.

Econometric Foundations of AI-Powered Structural Models

I aim to expand my foundational work on implicit regularization to build a comprehensive theoretical toolkit for validating next-generation structural models. This involves tackling the classic identification problem in the context of neural networks and generalizing the analysis of architectural bias to other advanced architectures, such as Transformers or Deep Sets.
